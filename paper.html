The Dataset

Raw scrape → cleaned feature set

The raw dataset contains 30,259 listings across 90 columns, scraped directly from live Airbnb listing pages (each row carries a listing_url and scrape_id), in the style of the well-known Inside Airbnb public data releases. It spans host metadata, property characteristics, coordinates, pricing, availability calendars, and review scores.

Three passes of feature pruning

Pass one removed identifiers, scrape metadata, and redundant host-listing-count aggregates (90 → 54 columns). Pass two dropped host fields with high missingness or weak signal — host_about, host_response_time, host_acceptance_rate — plus post-hoc review-timing fields (54 → 34). Pass three removed free text and duplicate geography: name, description, picture_url, and a redundant neighbourhood column that was 100% null. What remained was 24 structural features: location, room and property type, capacity, stay-length rules, host trust signals, and review scores.

Encoding & engineering

  • property_type (49 categories, from "Entire rental unit" to "Boat" and "Cave") and room_type (4 categories) were one-hot encoded.
  • host_identity_verified and has_availability were label encoded as binary/ternary flags.
  • amenities, stored as a JSON list per listing, was parsed and collapsed into a single amenities_count integer, then dropped in raw form.
  • price was stripped of currency symbols and thousands separators, then coerced to numeric.
  • Remaining numeric gaps (bathrooms, bedrooms, beds — each missing for roughly a third of rows) were filled with the column median; categorical gaps with the column mode.

Exploratory Analysis

Skew, correlation, and what actually moves price

Raw nightly price is heavily right-skewed — a mean near $248 against a standard deviation of $451 — driven by a long tail of luxury listings. Because squared-error losses are sensitive to that tail, a log1p transform was applied before modelling, producing a far more symmetric target and a more stable training signal.

Method

Splits, architecture, training

The 91-column feature matrix and log-price target were split 80% train, with the remainder split again into test and validation (≈80 / 16 / 4 overall), using a fixed seed for reproducibility. Features were standardized with a scaler fit only on the training partition.

91 → 64 → 32 → 16 → 1, ReLU between hidden layers. 8,513 trainable parameters.

  • Loss: Mean Squared Error
  • Optimizer: Adam, lr = 0.001
  • Batch size: 8, shuffled across all loaders
  • Epochs: 10
  • Target: trained on log1p(price), inverted with expm1 for dollar interpretation

Training loss fell from 0.796 to 0.218 over 10 epochs. Validation loss tracked closely, bottoming out at 0.188 around epoch 7 before drifting slightly upward — a mild early signal of overfitting, though the train/validation gap stayed small throughout.

An R² of 0.55 means the model explains just over half the variance in log-price using structural and locational features alone, with no text, image, or review-text signal included. On a single held-out listing used as a spot check, the model predicted ₹188.72 against an actual price of ₹126.87 — a useful reminder that aggregate metrics can look reasonable while individual predictions still carry real error.

5

Discussion

Why it plateaus where it does

Capacity and room configuration carry most of the learnable signal in a purely structural feature set, and performance under this feature set appears to plateau near R² ≈ 0.55. That's broadly consistent with other analyses of Inside-Airbnb-style data: structural features alone tend to leave real variance unexplained, with the rest tied up in things this dataset doesn't capture structurally — photo quality, reputation built into review text rather than numeric scores, seasonal demand, and competition among nearby listings.

The mild uptick in validation loss after epoch 7 suggests the network already has more capacity than 24 core structural features need — further gains are more likely to come from new feature sources than from a bigger model.

Limitations

  • All free text — listing name, description, host bio — was dropped early, despite plausibly carrying price signal.
  • Median/mode imputation was applied uniformly, even though bathrooms, bedrooms, and beds were each missing for roughly a third of listings.
  • A single fixed split and a fixed 10-epoch run were used — no k-fold cross-validation or learning-rate scheduling.
  • One-hot encoded property_type includes many rare categories ("Cave", "Boat", "Floor") the model can only weight unreliably.
6

Conclusion

End of the line

This project traced a full pipeline for NYC Airbnb price prediction: disciplined reduction of a 90-column raw scrape to 24 meaningful features, careful categorical encoding and skew correction, and a compact feed-forward network trained to an R² of 0.55 on unseen data. A large share of price variance is explainable from basic capacity, room type, and location alone — and the remainder is a clear invitation for future work: TF–IDF or embedding features from listing text, neighbourhood-level aggregate price features, and a head-to-head comparison against gradient-boosted trees (XGBoost, LightGBM), which are often competitive or better on tabular data like this.
